We are given data on house sales in years 2006-2010 in a U.S city, divided into training data sales and testing data sales_test, which contain 1584 and 350 samples, respectively. The data originally has 16 columns. We are going to model the data in the first column - sale_price (of a house) - using the other 15 features.

Using some plots and tools, we will now analyse the the features based on their values and the influence they have on the on the sale price, observe some relationships in between them and pre-process the data to get a better model.

First, we inspect whether there is any invalid data (NaN) and find out that our data is complete. Then we take a look at the skewness of the features in the columns. Since sale_price and lot_area are skewed the most, we can transform the data and create new columns with the $\log$ of the price and the lot area. However, the log of lot area gives the model fit a huge rmse, so we will take the log of the price only. Second, since the year a house was built or sold does not tell us much information (also, there is no correlation between the year and the price), and since we know from real life that age of the property matters, we will make a new column sale_age, being the age of the house at the point it was sold.

Numerical data

We can gain more insight looking at a correlation plot for the non-categorical data using biokit (documentation here), which displays the relationships nicely - blue/red is positive/negative correlation and the size and colour of the square is proportional to the size of the correlation.

  • The most remarkable correlations are between garage_cars and garage_area, and they also display similar correlation with other properties.
  • year_sold does not show correlation to any of the data. As one may have expected, the age has a negative correlation with the price (for regular houses, an old castle might be expensive though). Another interesting aspect we observe is that age is negatively correlated with garage area and number of car lots, also with basement area and number of bathrooms. The feature year_built has the opposite correlation to sale_age.
  • living_area is correlated strongly with sale_price which is understandable as well. Another features correlated with higher price are the number of full baths and garage area.
  • What might have not been expected is that lot_area neither bedrooms are correlated with the price much (0.29 and 0.19, respectively). We will, however, keep this data.

Non-numerical categorical data

The last 5 features in our data are non-numerical and categorical. Since we would like our data to be numerical, we will transform the categorical properties into numbers via One Hot Encoding using get_dummies and make separate columns with values 1 or 0 for specific values in them. To decide whether to include all of the columns we were given, we will make a few plots.

We start with the column neighborhood, and we would like to know whether a neighbourhood has an impact on the price - see the next boxplot. We notice, that some neighbourhoods, like nb_03 and nb_19, are more pricy then the others, especially nb_02. Therefore we decide to keep this data and unpack it with get_dummies later. However, if we unpacked all of the neighborhoods, we would get 24 columns/properties in total, which is quite a lot (especially in proportion to how much data we have in sales_test - we want to avoid under/overfitting). Instead, we will sort the neighborhoods by their mean price and make 3 groups: cheap, average and expensive neighborhoods (see the second boxplot).

In order to conclude whether and how air conditioning, ac, is important, we will make a swarmplot and use quality for the hue. We see that more houses had attribute Yes than No, and that very expensive houses (>270000 USD) all had ac. Average quality houses do not necessarily have air conditioning. Those houses of good and excellent quality, which are also among the more expensive ones, almost all have ac. Seeing this relationships we decide to include this data to make the model.

More on quality in the next two plots: The boxplot shows us that quality of the house decreases with age. There are also many outliers for good and average quality and older houses. In the swarmplot we can see direct relationship - sale price increasing in proportion to quality.

In a boxplot for zoning, we observe that the less dense areas are more expensive, so the zone of the house matters and we will not drop this data. However, only a small proportion of houses is in Medium and High density zones. Also, the zones' medians are quite close to each other. Overall, there is no strong trend present here.

The next violinplot and a set of swarmplots shows us the relationship between sales_price, quality and condition. Although from the violinplot it might seem that the rating of quality and condition of the house might be similar (because of similarly distributed data in each of the categories, compared to the previous swarmplot for quality), which would suggest we do not need both of these features, the swarmplots show us that it is not true. Quality=excellent is observed far more frequently with condition=average than with conition=excellent, although with higher price, and we get both excellent and poor condition with average quality. This means quality is not directly in relationship with condition and both columns are relevant.

3. Model Fitting and Tuning¶

After the analysis in section 1, we can now decide what variables to keep and which to drop.

Since we created a new column sale_age, after considering the correlations we will drop both year_sold, because it was decorrelated with other features, and year_built, because sale_age is capturing the opposite behaviour to year_built (it has the opposite correlation, because of the way the column was constructed).

Next, because of similar correlations of garage_cars and garage_area, we will therefore drop garage_cars, since the the area is enough for us and it might capture the price change better. Number of garage cars is categorical in nature, and with the same number of cars, garage area and price can vary (e.g. additional space in the garage for other stuff).

Because of not seeing a strong trend between zoning and the price, we will not take zoning into account.

Finally, since we transformed sale_price and lot_area by taking the logarithm, we will keep the new columns only and get rid of the old, skewed ones.

At the end we have 24 columns.

# helper functions taken from workshop 5
def get_coefs(m):
    """Returns the model coefficients from a Scikit-learn model object as an array,
    includes the intercept if available.
    """
    
    if m.intercept_ is None:
        return m.coef_
    
    return np.concatenate([[m.intercept_], m.coef_])

def model_fit(m, X, y, plot = False):
    """Returns the root mean squared error of a fitted model based on provided X and y values.
    
    Args:
        m: sklearn model object
        X: model matrix to use for prediction
        y: outcome vector to use to calculating rmse and residuals
        plot: boolean value, should fit plots be shown 
    """
    
    y_hat = np.exp(m.predict(X))
    rmse = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y, y_hat))

    
    res = pd.DataFrame(
        data = {'y': y, 'y_hat': y_hat, 'resid': y - y_hat}
    )
    
    if plot:
        plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
        
        plt.subplot(121)
        sns.lineplot(x='x', y='y', color="grey", data =  pd.DataFrame(data={'x': [min(y),max(y)], 'y': [min(y),max(y)]}))
        sns.scatterplot(x='y', y='y_hat', data=res).set_title("Fit plot")
        
        plt.subplot(122)
        sns.scatterplot(x='y', y='resid', data=res).set_title("Residual plot")
        
        plt.subplots_adjust(left=0.0)
        
        plt.suptitle("Model rmse = " + str(round(rmse, 4)), fontsize=16)
        plt.show()
    
    return rmse

RIDGE REGRESSION

After considering linear, lasso, and piecewise linear regression, and after comparing their RMSEs and residual plots, we settled for Ridge regression. Although linear regression gave us a slightly smaller RMSE, we chose Ridge because of its benefits (see the next section). The other two models had larger RMSEs. The residual plots are 'conic' for all of the methods - the models fit better the cheaper houses, than the more expensive ones (they are underpredicted). For comparison of the RMSEs we used data in sales_test.

To choose the right value of the penalization parameter alpha involved in Ridge Regression's Residual sum of squares, we perform Grid Search Cross-Validation using K-folds. First we tried to use 5 folds, but after running the code a couple of times, the best value of alpha varied with a big variance. We therefore increased the number of folds to 10 (we can do this, since our training data is large), and although the value still jumped, the variance decreased (the table demonstrates the situation).

4. Discussion¶

The model we chose to predict house prices is Ridge regression. Ridge regression is a regularization technique, that shrinks the estimated weights of the features. It was achieved by adding a penalty function $\alpha \sum^D_{d=1} w^2_d$ to Linear regression's cost function (RSS - Residual Sum of Squares), therefore it penalizes large weight values (weight as an importance of the feature to the predicted sale house). In total we have:

$RSS_{ridge} = \sum^N_{n=1} (y_n - w^Tx)n)^2 + \alpha \sum^D_{d=1} w^2_d $

This penalization helps our model avoid overfitting, since it optimizes for prediction, and may work better on the unseen data than linear regression, especially in a model complex as this one (we have many features).

In the previous section we found out that the best value for alpha is $\sim 5$ and the RMSE is $\sim$ 19 570 USD. We achieved this by first looking at the features of the houses and their interactions. We decided, that the number of cars that fit into a garage and the zoning would not be that important (garage cars because garage area has similar relationship to other features, zoning because there was not a strong trend visible with lower zoning resulting in higher price, the boxplot showed us that the ranges of the zones overlayed and their medians were close). Next, we divided the neighbourhoods into 3 groups of 8 based on their mean price:

  • cheap: 1, 2, 9, 6, 11, 13, 17, 23
  • medium: 4, 7, 8, 14, 18, 20, 22, 24
  • expensive: 3, 5, 10, 12, 15, 16, 19, 21

Also, we got more information about the house judging by its age at the year it was sold rather than the year it was built or sold. Hence we did not take the years into consideration and kept the age only.

The last important change we made was to take the log of price and the lot area, which made our modelling easier.

Since the input data for the linear regression is scaled, it is difficult to interpret the coefficients. To get an idea what is going on, we can take a look at the Linear Regression coefficients (they are not scaled) - the biggest weight here corresponds to quality_excellent.

5. Model Validation¶

The RMSE we got with Ridge regression is around 19 570 USD. Is this too much? It depends on the house itself. In the test data, the cheapest house costs 45 000 USD. In this case, the RMSE is almost half of the price of the house. For the expensive houses, however, 20k is not that much of a difference.

We can compute how much is this RMSE of the mean price of our training data, we get that it is around 11%. However, none of the models were able to obtain a better RMSE and have good prediction properties at the same time, so this option should be the best. Also, a reason for this RMSE being almost 20k might be financial crisis or the US housing bubble, peaking in 2006, which might have resulted in the data being inconsistent over the years (e.g. two houses with very similar features were sold for quite different prices in 2005 and 2010).

A drawback of this model can be in the training data being old - the moct recent year of a house being sold is 2010 - 10 years ago. For this or next year, a more recent data would be necessary to get a better model.
